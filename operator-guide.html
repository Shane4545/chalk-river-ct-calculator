CT is a short way of saying: how much chlorine is in the water × how long the water sits with it in the clearwell, pipes, and tower. This site is written for Ontario, Canada: the targets come from MECP’s disinfection procedure (under O. Reg. 170/03). The tool uses your flows, tank levels, pH, temperature, and chlorine readings to estimate whether you’re meeting those Ontario targets for Giardia (parasite cysts) and viruses.

Giardia — a bug that can make people sick; here it’s a score for chlorine vs parasite cysts, not a screen tag.
Viruses — a separate score for viruses; same general idea, different rules.

Stay safe: Only change the fields your procedure or supervisor says you may edit. If something is locked or looks like a table of numbers, leave it alone unless engineering tells you otherwise.

◈ Brief history — who, what, where, when, why

A short briefing for operators, engineers, and anyone new to the jargon. Official rules live with the province and your plant approvals; this is the story behind the CT check.

In plain language: Ontario law requires drinking-water systems to treat water properly and to be able to show that disinfection is doing its job. The province’s procedure tells plants to compare a calculated CT (chlorine × effective contact time) to published tables for Giardia cysts and viruses. The procedure expects that comparison to hold whenever the plant is running—so sites build operating habits (often each shift or day) around fresh flows, levels, pH, temperature, and chlorine, not because one magic calendar word is engraved in law, but because conditions change constantly.

Who

MECP (Ministry of the Environment, Conservation and Parks) publishes the Procedure for Disinfection of Drinking Water in Ontario, adopted by reference in O. Reg. 170/03. Engineers stamp site-specific models; operators run the plant and collect readings; the licence and your SOP say how often CT is calculated and logged.

What

The procedure’s appendix CT tables are the legal “ruler.” Your Chalk River Excel workbook encodes those tables into k factors and plant hydraulics. No single person “invented” the whole sheet—it's regulatory tables + engineering layout + spreadsheet logic.

Where

Laurentian Hills — Chalk River WTP is an Ontario system regulated under the Safe Drinking Water Act, 2002 and O. Reg. 170/03. The CT table numbers you work with are the ones MECP publishes in Ontario’s disinfection procedure (adopted by reference into law). Tank sizes, piping, flows, and baffling credits in the spreadsheet are site-specific to this plant.

When (Ontario)

After the Walkerton tragedy (May 2000), Ontario overhauled drinking-water law. The Safe Drinking Water Act, 2002 and O. Reg. 170/03 (in force 2003) tightened treatment, monitoring, and accountability. The current disinfection procedure (ontario.ca, updated August 2024 at time of writing) replaced older MOE guidance such as B13-3 (2001).

Why & where the math comes from

Waterborne outbreaks showed that filtration alone and weak chlorination are not enough. Regulators set health targets (for example high log-reductions for Giardia cysts and viruses through the treatment train) and turned lab and field kill data into CT tables by pH and temperature so every plant uses the same science.

Engineers: The legal source for the CT values used in Ontario is the Procedure for Disinfection of Drinking Water in Ontario on ontario.ca, incorporated into O. Reg. 170/03. Those tables reflect accepted disinfection science (chlorine, pH, temperature, and log-inactivation targets) that Canadian provinces apply in their own rules—your workbook encodes Ontario’s published tables. The alternate Giardia path (constants such as 0.2828, 2.69, 0.15, 0.933) is a standard engineering form seen in many Canadian plant calculation sheets; it is not unique to Chalk River.

Operators: the approved plant file + logbook win if anything disagrees. Engineers: verify against the current MECP procedure and your Director’s conditions.

More detail — Walkerton, “daily” calcs, and official links

Why Ontario tightened the screws: The Walkerton E. coli outbreak killed seven people and sickened thousands when contamination entered the system and chlorination was inadequate. Justice O’Connor’s public inquiry led to new legislation and technical rules so treatment and monitoring could not be treated casually. Archives Ontario hosts the inquiry records: https://www.archives.gov.on.ca/en/e_records/walkerton/

Does the law say “every day”? O. Reg. 170/03 and the procedure require compliance at all times during operation and continuous / prescribed monitoring depending on your system category. Plants typically run CT worksheets every operating day or shift because flows and chemistry change—that’s operational good practice and what many licences and SOPs expect, even if your exact frequency is set by your approval.

Who invented CT? The CT idea (concentration × time) comes from long-standing disinfection kinetics used in public health engineering worldwide. The exact numbers in your shift work are not invented at the plant: they are the values Ontario fixed in its legal procedure after setting health targets (how many logs of Giardia cyst and virus inactivation treatment must achieve). For “what law do I follow?” the answer is Ontario—Safe Drinking Water Act, 2002, O. Reg. 170/03, and the disinfection procedure on ontario.ca.

R Laws, regulations & penalties

What legally backs disinfection and treatment in Ontario, and what the Safe Drinking Water Act, 2002 says about maximum court penalties. This is not legal advice—actual outcomes depend on facts, prosecution, and the court.

Statute & main regulations

  • Safe Drinking Water Act, 2002 (S.O. 2002, c. 32) — parent law for drinking-water systems, licences, offences, administrative penalties, and ministry powers.
  • O. Reg. 170/03 — Drinking-Water Systems (treatment categories, operational requirements, and adoption by reference of the Procedure for Disinfection of Drinking Water in Ontario, which holds the CT tables your plant uses).
  • O. Reg. 169/03 — Ontario Drinking Water Quality Standards (microbiological, chemical, and radiological standards for water supplied to users).
  • O. Reg. 128/04 — Certification of drinking-water system operators and water quality analysts (competence, training, and related operational rules tied to certificates).
  • O. Reg. 242/05 — Compliance and enforcement (inspections, mandatory follow-up when deficiencies are found, reporting timelines in defined cases).

Other regulations and your licence, permit, or Director’s orders can add site-specific duties (monitoring frequency, corrective actions). A CT worksheet supports proof of disinfection performance; violating treatment rules, licence conditions, orders, or reporting duties is where enforcement usually attaches—not a single arithmetic mistake read in isolation.

Enforcement tools (overview)

MECP can use inspections, Director’s orders, licence conditions, requirements for operational plans, and court applications to restrain contraventions. The Act also allows administrative penalties (orders to pay money for prescribed contraventions, without a full court trial process in the usual sequence) and provincial offence prosecutions leading to fines or jail within the ranges below. Officers, directors, employees, or agents can be prosecuted when they commit acts for which a corporation would be liable.

Summarized from sections 141–143 of the Safe Drinking Water Act, 2002. Amounts are per day or part of a day the offence occurs or continues unless the section says otherwise. “Subsequent conviction” depends on prior convictions listed in the Act (including certain other environmental statutes—see section 144).

Administrative penalties: Under section 121, an order may impose a penalty up to $100,000 per contravention (plus possible disgorgement of economic benefit), as further detailed in regulations made under the Act. This is separate from court fines above.

Victim fine surcharge: Convictions under provincial offence procedures may also attract a surcharge on top of the fine (see the Provincial Offences Act and current surcharge rules).

Published examples: Ontario has reported SDWA prosecutions with fines in the tens of thousands of dollars (for example reporting and operational failures)—well below the statutory maximums. Search the Ontario Newsroom or court bulletins for current cases; they are illustrations only.

Disclaimer: Penalties apply to specific contraventions proved in law. They are not automatic because a training-page CT value disagrees with the spreadsheet. For any inspection, order, or charge, follow your utility’s legal counsel and MECP direction.

5 Parts of the CT tool (main file)

You type here Auto-calculated
Inputs — where operators type readings
This is where you enter flows (L/s), free chlorine (mg/L), pH, temperature (°C), how full tanks are (%), and baffling factors. You may also set targets for Giardia and viruses if your procedure says so. Names like FIT-, FRC-, PH- usually match what you see on SCADA.
Results — read only
Shows each chunk of the plant (clearwell, pipe, tower): how much water, how long it sits, effective “contact” time after baffling, and scores for Giardia and viruses. The bottom summary is what most people check first. There may be a forecast block—use it only if your SOP says so.
Lookup tables (Giardia & viruses)
Big tables of official-style numbers the tool looks up automatically. Do not edit unless an engineer tells you to after a formal change.
Notes & assumptions
Explains limits, warnings, and history. Worth reading once; skim again after any update. Some displays cap high scores as “>3” or “>4” instead of showing a bigger number.

IN What goes where (typical layout)

Labels often match Chalk River SCADA; order on screen can vary slightly.

Elevated water tower
Usually how full the tank is (%) and how fast water is moving (L/s). Mixing / baffling changes how much “sitting time” really counts.
Clearwell
How much water is in the clearwell depends on level. Flow often uses the higher of two meters where the model asks for a pair. Low baffling = water shortcuts through = less contact credit.
Chlorine & water quality
Free chlorine (mg/L), pH, and temperature at the sampling points your procedure names. The tool rounds some values to match the official tables.
Targets (what the permit asks for)
The targets your permit asks for (Giardia and viruses) decide how required contact compares to what you actually have on the summary.

OUT Reading the results

  • Contact × chlorine (achieved) — how much “chlorine power × time” you actually have through the clearwell, pipe, and tower.
  • Contact × chlorine (required) — what the rules say you need, based on your targets and the scores shown.
  • Giardia / virus scores — may show > 3 or > 4 when the display tops out; the real value can be higher.
If numbers look wrong, first check flow = 0 anywhere it should not be, pH/temperature in realistic ranges, and baffling factors against your approved study or SOP.

★ Operator tips

  • Fill in the input section top to bottom each shift (or whenever your SOP says)—don’t skip flows.
  • When a tank is moving a lot, update both how full it is and flow for the moment you’re checking.
  • If files were ever copied from another plant, confirm tank sizes and tag names with engineering.
  • Keep a copy of the tool’s notes / assumptions page handy for limits and special cases.

? FAQ

Why does the math use two ways to score the same step?
The model often takes the stricter (higher) of two approved methods so you don’t accidentally claim too much credit. Day to day, trust the summary at the bottom, not every middle column.
What is “effective contact time”?
Roughly: how long water sits in that tank or pipe × a baffling factor. Poor mixing (low baffling) means water shortcuts through—you get less credit for “sitting time.”
Can I change locked or calculated areas?
Only if your site’s change control allows it. If numbers look wrong, write down your readings and call engineering or water quality.
